Tanking in the NBA

What is tanking?

Tanking occurs when teams that are not going to make the playoffs intentionally start trying to lose so that they have a higher probability of getting better draft picks. In the NBA, teams with the worst record have the highest likelihood of receiving the first draft pick, which means they would be able to draft the best players from college. This would help a team in the rebuilding phase. A summary of the NBA Draft Lottery can be found here: https://www.nba.com/nba-draft-lottery-explainer

The NBA has been attempting to curb tanking by changing rules that may provide benefit to lower performing teams. Typically, teams with lower records earn a higher draft pick. Therefore, the NBA instituted a lottery based system that does not make it deterministic that the lowest standing team in the NBA receives the first (best) pick in the NBA draft: https://www.si.com/nba/2019/05/15/nba-draft-lottery-lebron-james-anthony-davis-lakers-pelicans-tanking-not-dead

In order to tank and lose, teams sometimes choose to trade away their good players, play their younger/worse players more often to get them more experience. In recent years, teams have openly acknowledged tanking, such as the Dallas Mavericks in 2018 (https://twitter.com/ESPNNBA/status/965979545856806914). This past year tanking has been an even bigger point of discussion as several teams, most notably the New York Knicks, have been losing with the goal of getting the first pick and the top player in the draft, Zion Williamson: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zouIqKN8rZA.

Project Motivation

Because tanking has become such a common point of discussion over the past year, we thought it would be interesting to attempt to quantify tanking in the NBA to determine if teams were making a deliberate attempt to lose. This information would be especially important to know for the NBA to decide whether or not they should change rules or fine teams for tanking during the season. We decided to use not this seasons but the previous season (2017-2018) data since the current season is ongoing and the previous years data is more complete. \

In order to do so, we decided to look at primarily two variables: how strong was a teams lineup and their winning percentage throughout the season. We hypothesize that teams with a low winning percentage will have a declining strength in their team lineup as they try to tank. \

Although winning percentage can easily be calculated based on a teams win and loss record, we had to design a metric to quantify how good a teams lineup was. This also meant we needed to have a way to quantify how good players in a lineup were. The folllowing tutorial will explain our data processing to make these calculations and overall measure the relationship between win percentage and strength of lineup.

To measure overall player rating, we decided to use the video game NBA 2K18s player ratings. NBA 2K18 is highly respected for the ratings that it reveals each year with players obsessed about how the game judges their gameplay. Joining the Tables

To merge the two tables, we performed a left join on the data. However, we quickly noticed that we needed to clean the data. The box scores were not able to represent accents and other such characters or if a player had an alternate name (nickname) or spelling which caused the values to be incorrect and result in a player not being matched to a rating.

One limitation in the NBA 2K ratings dataset is that it did not include the ratings of free agent players. During the NBA season, teams commonly sign these players. To find the free agents ratings, we used https://www.2kratings.com/ which has the ratings for NBA2k19. Although the game is of this year instead of last, free agents ratings typically do not fluctuate much so we found it acceptable to use these values. We manually added these values to the NBA 2K Ratings table before performing the join.

Player Weighting

We decided to multiply a players rating by the number of minutes that they played in a game to determine how much they contribute in value to a team. Then for each teams game on a given date, we summed up each players weighted rating to determine the overall team rating for that game.

teamstrength <- merged %>%
  mutate(WeightedRating = Minutes*Rating, DateFormat=as.Date(Date, "%m/%d/%Y")) %>%
  group_by(DateFormat, Team) %>%
  summarize(GameTeamStrength=sum(WeightedRating))

Team Lineup Strength Overview

Based on the graphs presented above, we believe the majority of teams that have curves that decline significantly over the course of the season represent teams that could potentially be tanking since the curve decreasing means that they are playing their worse players more than their star players compared to what they usually did. In a tanking scenario, this decline should begin around February since that indicates the halfway point in the season. At this point, teams will know whether or not they have a legitimate chance of making the playoffs and can readjust their goals for the season.

There are some limitations to that analysis however. For some teams, it was not a deliberate attempt to lose but sometimes their star players got injured causing the curve to decline. For example, the Washington Wizards declined because of the injury to John Wall, the New York Knicks lost Kristaps Porzingis, and the Memphis Grizzlies lost Mike Conley. Additionally, teams like the Golden State Warriors choose to rest star players towards the end of the season once they have clinched a spot in the playoffs.

Sometimes the graphs went upwards unexpectedly because of trades midseason or free agent signings. Dwyane Wade was signed by the Miami Heat, the Pistons received Blake Griffin (trade) which caused their team strength value to increase.

However, by analyzing individual box scores from midseason to the end of the season, we can see that some other players may have gotten more minutes.

Based on the information of a declining lineup and poor standing, we believe that the following teams could potentially be tanking:
